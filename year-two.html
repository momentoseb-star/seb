UX design Jakobs law​

Jakob's Law is a principle of user experience (UX) design, named after Jakob Nielsen, a well-known usability expert. The law states:​

"Users spend most of their time on other websites, so they prefer your site to work the same way as all the other sites they already know."​

Key Takeaways:​

Consistency in design: Users expect websites and apps to follow familiar patterns they've encountered elsewhere. When designers follow standard conventions, it reduces the user's cognitive load, helping them navigate and interact more efficiently.​ Reduce the learning curve: If a website behaves in unexpected ways, users may become frustrated. By adhering to common design standards, designers can make it easier for users to understand and use the site without needing to relearn new patterns.​ Familiarity breeds comfort: People are more likely to engage with and trust a site that feels intuitive because it aligns with their past experiences. Following familiar design patterns (like where the navigation bar or search function is placed) enhances the overall user experience.​

Application in UX:​

Designers often apply Jakob's Law by leveraging widely accepted design elements, such as placing navigation menus at the top or on the left side, using consistent icons (like a magnifying glass for search), and following other standard user interface (UI) conventions. This approach helps meet user expectations and improves usability.​

In short, Jakob's Law emphasizes designing for user familiarity, ensuring ease of use by following established standards.

    Brand developments

  • Brand Strategy: The foundation of brand development starts with a well-defined brand strategy. This involves understanding the brand's purpose, mission, values, and target audience, as well as setting long-term goals.​
  • Brand Identity: This includes the visual and verbal elements that represent the brand, such as the name, logo, color palette, typography, and tone of voice. Consistency in brand identity helps build recognition
  • Brand Positioning: Defining how the brand is perceived in relation to competitors. It focuses on creating a unique value proposition that sets the brand apart in the minds of consumers.
  • Brand Communication: This involves the messaging and channels used to communicate the brand's value and identity to the audience. It includes advertising, digital marketing, public relations, and social media.
  • Customer Experience: Ensuring that customers’ interactions with the brand, from first exposure to post-purchase experiences, align with the brand’s promises. Great customer experiences enhance brand loyalty.
  • Brand Equity: Over time, successful brand development builds brand equity— the perceived value and emotional connection that customers have with the brand. High brand equity leads to trust, customer retention, and the ability to charge premium prices.
  • Innovation and Growth: Brands evolve by innovating new products or services, expanding into new markets, or adapting to changing customer needs. This is an essential part of long-term brand development.

    Bandai how they create characters and product

    Concept Design​

  • Collaboration with Licensors: Bandai works closely with Toei Animation and Shueisha, the companies behind Dragon Ball, to ensure the figures match the official character designs and respect the intellectual property.
  • Concept Art: Artists and designers create detailed concept art for the figure. This includes defining the character’s pose, facial expressions, accessories, and any special features like energy effects or battle damage.​
  • Reference Material: They reference anime/manga frames, 3D models, or other media to ensure accuracy.

    • 3D Sculpting and Modeling

  • 3D Modeling: Using software like ZBrush or Maya, artists create a 3D model of the character based on the concept art. This involves sculpting the character’s features, clothing, and accessories in a digital environment.
  • Texturing: Once the 3D model is complete, artists apply textures and colors to the model to match the character’s design. This includes adding details like skin tones, clothing patterns, and other surface details.
  • Rendering: The final step involves rendering the 3D model to create high-quality images or animations for promotional materials and packaging.
  • Pose Selection: At this stage, the team decides on a dynamic or iconic pose for the character. For example, Goku might be sculpted in his Kamehameha pose or Vegeta in a battle-ready stance.
  • Prototyping: A prototype is 3D-printed, allowing the design team to physically inspect the figure and adjust.

    • Approval and Refinement​

  • Licensor Approval: The prototype is sent for review and approval by Toei or Shueisha. They ensure the figure aligns with Dragon Ball’s official representation.​
  • Refinement: Based on feedback, adjustments are made to improve accuracy, such as tweaking facial expressions, colors, or details like hair spikes or armor pieces.​

    • Mold Creation

  • Mold Development: Once the design is finalized, steel molds are created. These molds are used to mass-produce the figure’s parts, usually made from plastic typically PVC or ABS plastics.
  • Precision: The molds are finely crafted to capture intricate details such as hair strands, facial features, and costume textures and muscle definition.​

    • Injection Molding and Casting​

  • Injection Molding: Molten plastic is injected into the molds to create the figure's individual parts. Each piece is produced separately, such as the head, arms, legs, and any accessories weapons, energy effects.​
  • Material Selection: Different types of plastics or vinyl are used depending on the flexibility and strength required for each part, softer plastic for capes or accessories, harder plastic for structural component.​

    • Assembly​

  • Figure Assembly: After molding, the various parts are carefully assembled by hand or by machines. Some figures have interchangeable parts (for example, different hands or heads), requiring multiple pieces to be joined.​
  • Articulation: If the figure is articulated like the S.H. Figuarts, the joints are designed to allow movement without breaking the sculpt’s aesthetics and silhouette.​

    • Painting and Detailing

  • Hand-Painting: Skilled workers hand-paint the finer details such as facial expressions, shading on muscles, and special effects like, glowing eyes . Automated machines and processes are used for larger or repetitive areas.​
  • Color Application: Advanced techniques like tampo printing or airbrushing are employed for consistent application of colors, particularly for difficult areas like eyes or small accessories.​
  • Battle Effects: Some figures feature battle damage or energy effects, which are often highlighted through specialpaint applications or transparent plastic pieces.​​

    • Quality Control

  • Inspection: Each figure undergoes a quality control process to ensure all parts fit together properly, colors are accurate, and the overall figure meets the desired standards.​
  • Test Assembly: Articulated figures are tested for pose ability, ensuring joints work correctly without being too loose or stiff.

    • Packaging and distribution

  • Box Design: The packaging is designed with Dragon Ball’s signature aesthetic, often featuring character artwork, logos, and clear windows to showcase the figure inside.​
  • Final Inspection: Before shipping, figures are inspected one final time to check for any imperfections in painting or assembly. Approved figures are placed in blister packaging or boxes and readied for distribution.
  • Global Shipping: Once packaged, the figures are distributed worldwide to retailers, where they are sold to collectors and fans.​
  • Limited Editions: Bandai also releases special editions or premium versions of figures, which may include extra accessories, alternative colors, or exclusive packaging.​

    • Special Figure Lines by Bandai:

    • S.H. Figuarts: Known for their highly articulated, dynamic pose ability and detailed sculpts, these are aimed at adult collectors.
    • Dragon Stars Series: A more budget-friendly line with slightly less articulation and detail but still high-quality.
    • Tamashii Nations: Bandai’s premium brand, featuring exclusive figures with high-end finishes and extra accessories.
    • Banpresto Prize Figures: These are affordable, non-articulated figures, often won in Japanese crane games and are popular with casual fans.

I was able to complete the bust, and overall, I’m really happy with the outcome. It gave me a better understanding of form, depth, and how character designs work in a physical space.
